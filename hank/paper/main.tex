\begin{abstract}
We introduce \SWIFTbot, a pipeline for discovering efficient gate sets for fault-tolerant quantum computation. It operates in two modes. In \emph{universal} mode, it ranks $(\mathcal{C},g)$ pairs --- a finite base group $\mathcal{C}\subset\mathrm{SU}(d)$ extended by a single gate $g$ --- by the Quantum Circuit Overhead $\QT$, the asymptotic cost of approximating Haar-uniform targets. In \emph{application-specific} mode, it minimizes the mean synthesis depth over a structured target family picked out by the application. A third stage matches surviving pairs against a curated catalog of transversal QEC codes, magic-state distillation/cultivation/code-switching protocols, and certified exact-synthesis rings, flagging which pairs admit an end-to-end fault-tolerant stack. At $d=3$ the universal mode yields the first systematic $\QT$ ranking across three $\Sigma$-series subgroups, with arbitrary-SU(3) extensions on the largest approaching the Kesten--McKay floor within $32\%$, while structured diagonal and Howard--Vala extensions sit $3$--$70\times$ above. In application-specific mode, a non-canonical rational-phase extension beats canonical Clifford$+T$ by $6.8\times$ on a lattice-gauge target family, showing that the best general-purpose and best target-specific extensions can differ by nearly an order of magnitude. The pipeline, registered groups, fault-tolerance catalog, and reproduction data are released with the paper.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Fault-tolerant quantum computation requires a finite universal gate set whose members have cheap physical realizations. The tension between these requirements is sharp: the Eastin--Knill theorem~\cite{EastinKnill:2009} forbids any non-trivial quantum error-correcting code from admitting a universal \emph{transversal} gate set. Practical designs therefore split the gate set into a transversal subgroup $\mathcal{C}\subset\mathrm{SU}(d)$ that the error-correcting code protects cheaply, plus one or more non-transversal extension gates $g$ made fault-tolerant by any of several routes: magic-state distillation~\cite{Bravyi:2004isx,Bravyi:2012czv}, magic-state cultivation~\cite{Gidney:2024cultivation}, code switching~\cite{OuyangJingBrennen:2024}, or beyond-stabilizer codes with native non-Clifford transversal action~\cite{Kubischta:2I,DenysLeverrier:2023,DenysLeverrier:2024}. Each extension gate $g$ comes with its own cost: a dedicated magic state, a code that supports it transversally, a distillation or cultivation circuit, or some combination of the above.

\paragraph*{Why qudits.}
The qubit version of this story has a standard resolution: Clifford$+T$~\cite{Kitaev:1997wr,Bravyi:2004isx}, with Clifford transversal on Steane, Reed--Muller, and Bravyi--Haah families and $T$ distilled via triorthogonal MSD~\cite{Bravyi:2004isx,Bravyi:2012czv}; single-qubit synthesis into this gate set is solved with certified $T$-count bounds~\cite{Ross:2014kbr,Kliuchnikov:2012pla}. This toolkit is mature but not closed: as we show for the binary icosahedral base $2I$ (\cref{sec:d2}), systematic $(\mathcal{C},g)$ exploration beyond the canonical choice remains productive even at $d=2$, and four developments motivate extending the search into qudit registers at $d>2$.

Qudit hardware has matured on several fronts. Trapped-ion platforms have demonstrated single-qudit registers up to $d=7$~\cite{Ringbauer:2022qudit} and operate multi-ion chains at lower $d$; superconducting cavities encode $\gtrsim\!10$ Fock levels per mode with coherence times approaching the best qubit counterparts~\cite{Kim:2025multiqudit}; reconfigurable neutral-atom arrays provide individually addressable sites at the hundred-qudit scale~\cite{Pecorari:2025natom}; and solid-state spin-$S$ systems offer $d=2S+1$ native encodings~\cite{Wang:2020qudits,Kiktenko:2024colloquium}. The binary-encoding overhead qubits impose on inherently higher-$d$ physics is a cost, not a feature of these architectures.

Such hardware development has been matched by advances in theoretical proposals for fault tolerance, both on the QEC side and on the non-transversal-gate side.

Qudit codes have progressed beyond the qubit toolkit. Gottesman's prime-$d$ stabilizer construction~\cite{Gottesman:1998} spelled out the foundational split from the qubit case; beyond stabilizer, the \emph{nonadditive} Kubischta $((7,2,3))_{2I}$ code~\cite{Kubischta:2I} and Denys--Leverrier \emph{covariant} $2T$/$2O$ codes~\cite{DenysLeverrier:2023,DenysLeverrier:2024} carry transversal non-Clifford groups that sidestep the Anderson--Jochym-O'Connor qubit-stabilizer no-go~\cite{Anderson:2014}. HEP-motivated bosonic proposals such as the $2T$-quoctit encode specific discrete subgroups natively at $d=8$~\cite{Kurkcuoglu:2025quoctit}. Non-local qLDPC memories like the bivariate-bicycle code~\cite{Bravyi:2024bb} need connectivity graphs whose natural hardware substrates --- the same platforms above --- make qudit qLDPC codes~\cite{Spencer:2025qldpc,Vasic:2025qldpc} a convergence rather than two separate programs.

Distillation and cultivation are advancing in parallel. Qutrit triorthogonal distillation~\cite{Campbell:2012jna,PrakashSaha:2025} reaches $\gamma\approx 1.51$, beating qubit triorthogonal codes below $\sim\!300$ physical qudits; asymptotically-good qudit constructions~\cite{WillsHsiehYamasaki:2025,Cervia:2025agc} deliver constant-overhead MSD. Magic-state cultivation~\cite{Gidney:2024cultivation,Vaknin:2025cultivation,Rosenfeld:2025cultivation} is currently qubit-native and surface-code-specific, and an open qudit analogue would close one of the two remaining arguments for $d=2$ canonicalization (\cref{sec:discussion_ft}).

Applications drive the practical case for qudits. Lattice-gauge simulations of $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ live on $|\mathcal{C}|$-dimensional per-link registers that fit qudit architectures natively ($|\Sigma(36\!\times\!3)|=108$ states per link, encoded on three qutrits plus two qubits~\cite{Lamm:2019bik,GustafsonJi:2024sig}); experimental trapped-ion realizations of $\mathrm{U}(1)$ and $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ gauge dynamics already use native qudits~\cite{Meth:2023wzd,Popov:2023tkp}, and native-qudit QEC constructions are in active development for $\mathbb{Z}_N$~\cite{Spagnoli:2026qsc} and $\mathrm{SU}(2)$~\cite{Bradshaw:2026aec} lattice gauge theories. Beyond lattice gauge, higher-$d$ registers reduce the Trotter cost of continuous-variable bosonic simulations, cut the ancilla overhead of fermion-orbital encodings in chemistry and materials science, and absorb large-spin degrees of freedom (magnetism, $S>\tfrac{1}{2}$ impurities) without binary unpacking~\cite{Wang:2020qudits,Kiktenko:2024colloquium}.

\paragraph*{qLDPC codes and qudit extensions.}
A complementary insight: modern codes like qLDPC may be better understood as qudit codes than as collections of qubit codes. The gate set accessible on a $[[n,k,d]]$ qLDPC code like the $[[144,12,12]]$ gross code~\cite{Bravyi:2024bb} through fold-transversal Cliffords and code automorphisms is \emph{not} the product group $\mathrm{Clifford}(2)^{\otimes k}$: recent work on bivariate-bicycle codes shows the fold-transversal Cliffords act collectively on the logical qubits~\cite{Eberhardt:2024ft}, with the general code-automorphism-to-logical-gate construction~\cite{Sayginel:2024ca} and the remaining-logical-Cliffords syndrome-extraction cost~\cite{Malcolm:2025qldpc} now spelled out. Built from code-structured Pauli-string operators, the accessible set is more naturally read as a specific finite subgroup $\mathcal{C}\subset\mathrm{SU}(2^k)$ --- a single-qudit object at $d=2^k$ rather than a tensor product of qubit gates. The corresponding non-Clifford completion could then be a single unitary $g$ on the $k$-qubit logical space: a high-weight entangling gate in the qubit basis, but a single-qudit extension at $d=2^k$. Distilling the one $|\psi_g\rangle$ magic state is structurally distinct from distilling $k$ separate $|T\rangle$'s, and potentially cheaper: constant-rate qudit distillation~\cite{WillsHsiehYamasaki:2025,Cervia:2025agc} makes this a live possibility rather than a formal rephrasing. The $(\mathcal{C},g)$ ranking this paper formalizes applies once $\mathcal{C}$ is concretely identified and the relevant $t$-design irreps at $d=2^k$ are tractable; a gross-code case study is left to future work.

No comparable consensus exists at $d>2$. A three-parameter family $\HV{z}{\gamma}{\varepsilon}$ of qudit $T$-gates at prime $d$ is known~\cite{Howard:2012fva}, but no systematic comparison of members has been done. The finite-subgroup classification of $\mathrm{SU}(d)$ is complete for $d \leq 4$~\cite{Blichfeldt:1917,Fairbairn:1964,Flyvbjerg:1985,HananyHe:2001,GrimusLudl:2010} but open beyond, and transversal-gate classifications for stabilizer codes are available for qubit stabilizer codes~\cite{Anderson:2014}; qudit analogues exist for specific code families but not in the same exhaustive form. Prime-$d$ distillation protocols exist~\cite{Campbell:2012jna}, qutrit synthesis for Shor-class algorithms has been studied~\cite{Bocharov:2017qutrit}, and Ross--Selinger-style exact synthesis has recently been extended to Clifford$+R$ at $d=3$~\cite{Gustafson:2025qutrit}. What is missing is a quantitative ranking: given a base group $\mathcal{C}$ and an extension $g$, how efficiently does $(\mathcal{C},g)$ synthesize target unitaries?

S{\l}owik, Duli{\'a}n, and Sawicki~\cite{Slowik:2025qco} supplied the right figure of merit. The \emph{Quantum Circuit Overhead} $\QT(\mathcal{C},g)$ is the asymptotic exponent in the word length of an $\varepsilon$-accurate approximation: the number of gates scales as $(1/\varepsilon)^{\QT}$, bounded below by the Kesten--McKay optimum $\Popt$ and saturated by the ``golden gates'' of Sarnak~\cite{Sarnak:2015}.

This paper uses $\QT$ to rank candidates. We introduce \SWIFTbot, an agentic workflow that enumerates $(\mathcal{C},g)$ pairs --- in principle for any dimension $d$, tested in the present paper at $d\leq 4$ where classification bounds are known --- screens each for universality via the Sawicki--Karnas criterion, evaluates $\QT$ numerically~\cite{Slowik:2025qco}, and cross-references surviving pairs against a curated catalog of $12$ transversal QEC codes (stabilizer and beyond-stabilizer), $10$ magic-state distillation / cultivation / code-switching protocols spanning five target-gate families, and two certified exact-synthesis rings.

We make three contributions. First, at $d=2$ we reproduce the S{\l}owik \emph{et al.} numerics at the percent level (\cref{sec:d2}). Second, we establish the first systematic $\QT$ ranking at $d=3$ across the three irreducible-adjoint $\Sigma$-series subgroups with $|\mathcal{C}|\in\{216,648,1080\}$, using Howard--Vala, rational-phase, and arbitrary-SU(3) extensions (\cref{sec:d3}). Third, applied to a gate set relevant for lattice-gauge theory simulation~\cite{Lamm:2019bik,Gustafson:2022xdt,Kurkcuoglu:2025quoctit}, a non-canonical rational-phase extension beats Clifford$+T$ by $6.8\times$ in mean synthesis depth (\cref{sec:lamm}). Together these show that the best general-purpose extension and the best target-specific extension can differ by nearly an order of magnitude---a distinction invisible when $\QT$ alone is reported.

Code and data are released with this paper~\cite{swiftbot_github}. \cref{sec:qco} reviews the QCO framework, \cref{sec:methods} sketches the pipeline, \cref{sec:d2,sec:d3,sec:lamm} present the results, and \cref{sec:discussion} closes with a fault-tolerance audit and open problems.

\section{Background}\label{sec:qco}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We briefly review the three ingredients used throughout: the Quantum Circuit Overhead $\QT$~\cite{Slowik:2025qco} as our primary figure of merit, the Sawicki--Karnas criterion~\cite{Sawicki:2016kmo} for ruling out non-universal extensions without running QCO, and the Howard--Vala family~\cite{Howard:2012fva} and discrete-subgroup-approximation (DSA) primitive-gate series~\cite{Lamm:2019bik,Gustafson:2022xdt} as the principal sources of candidate extensions at $d \geq 3$.

\subsection{$\delta$-approximate $t$-designs and $\QT$}\label{sec:qco_qt}

The Quantum Circuit Overhead $\QT$ is a property of a \emph{generating set} $\mathcal{S} = \mathcal{C}\cup\{g, g^{-1}\}$, not of the base group $\mathcal{C}$ alone; a poor choice of extension $g$ can make the Cayley random walk converge slowly on $\mathrm{SU}(d)$ even on a base group with a large spectral gap. We therefore never quote $\QT$ without specifying the extension.

Let $\mathcal{S} = \mathcal{C} \cup \mathcal{C}^{-1} \cup \{g,g^{-1}\} \subset \mathrm{SU}(d)$ be a symmetric generating set consisting of a finite subgroup $\mathcal{C}$ and a single extension $g$. Consider the $t$-design operator on the $t$-th tensor power of the defining representation,
\begin{equation}
  T_t(\mathcal{S}) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}|} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \pi_t(s)\,, \qquad \pi_t = \bigoplus_{\lambda \in \Lambda_t} \pi_\lambda^{\oplus m_\lambda}\,,
  \label{eq:tdesign}
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda_t$ is the set of $\mathrm{SU}(d)$ highest weights that appear in the decomposition of $(\mathbb{C}^d)^{\otimes t} \otimes (\mathbb{C}^d)^{*\otimes t}$ and $\pi_\lambda$ is the associated irreducible representation. The \emph{$t$-design spectral radius} is
\begin{equation}
  \delta_t(\mathcal{S}) \;=\; \max_{\lambda \in \Lambda_t \setminus \{0\}} \bigl\lVert T_t(\mathcal{S})\bigr\rVert_{\pi_\lambda}\,,
  \label{eq:delta_def}
\end{equation}
the largest spectral norm across non-trivial irreps. A smaller $\delta_t$ means the random walk generated by $\mathcal{S}$ equidistributes faster on $\mathrm{SU}(d)$.

For an $\varepsilon$-net of $\mathrm{SU}(d)$ in the operator norm, Słowik \emph{et al.}~\cite{Slowik:2025qco} show that the number of Solovay--Kitaev-style words required scales as $(1/\varepsilon)^{\QT}$, with
\begin{equation}
  \QT(\mathcal{S}) \;=\; \frac{\log|\mathcal{C}|}{\log\bigl(\delta_t^{-1}(\mathcal{S})\bigr)}\,,
  \label{eq:QT}
\end{equation}
in the limit $t \to \infty$. The Kesten--McKay bound~\cite{Kesten:1959symmetric,McKay:1981eigenvalue,Lubotzky:1994expander} for a $k$-regular Cayley graph gives, in the $|\mathcal{C}|\to\infty$ asymptotic we use throughout,
\begin{equation}
  \delta_t \;\geq\; \delta_\mathrm{opt} \;=\; \frac{2\sqrt{|\mathcal{C}|-1}}{|\mathcal{C}|}\,,
  \label{eq:delta_opt}
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}
  \QT \;\geq\; \Popt \;=\; \frac{\log|\mathcal{C}|}{\log(1/\delta_\mathrm{opt})}\,,
  \label{eq:Popt}
\end{equation}
with equality for ``golden gates''~\cite{Sarnak:2015}. \cref{tab:popt} lists $\Popt$ for every base group in our registry that we use as a baseline below.

In practice $\QT$ is estimated at finite $t$ by sampling the ensemble in \cref{eq:tdesign} with a Monte-Carlo extension gate $g$ (the \emph{rnd} baseline) or a deterministic one, then evaluating \cref{eq:delta_def} via sparse spectral decomposition on each irrep of $\pi_t$. The feasibility ceiling at $d=3$ is set by representation dimensions~\cite{Slowik:2025qco}: the largest irrep at $t = 50$ has dimension $\sim 10^5$, whereas $t = 15$ peaks at $\sim 4\times 10^3$ and is tractable. At $d=2$, the S{\l}owik~\emph{et al.}\ $\QT(t)$ convergence curves~\cite{Slowik:2025qco} reach stabilization at the percent level by $t\gtrsim 30$. At $d=3$ our reference point is weaker: we observe the Tier-1 ($t=5$) and Tier-2 ($t=10$) $\delta$ values agree to four decimals for all structured extensions (\cref{sec:d3}), which strongly suggests stabilization, but without an independent $t\to\infty$ reference we cannot tightly bound the residual $\QT$ error at $d=3$. An asymptotic extrapolation $\QT(t\to\infty)$ from a $\QT(t)$ sweep is a natural next step. We quote $\Popt$ alongside $\QT$ throughout to expose the gap to optimality.

\subsection{Pre-screening with the Sawicki--Karnas criterion}\label{sec:qco_sawicki}

Not every candidate extension needs a QCO run. Sawicki and Karnas~\cite{Sawicki:2016kmo} proved that $\mathcal{S}$ generates a dense subgroup of $\mathrm{SU}(d)$ if and only if
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=*]
  \item the adjoint representation of $\mathcal{S}$ is irreducible, and
  \item $\langle\mathcal{S}\rangle$ --- the subgroup generated by $\mathcal{S}$ --- is not contained in any finite subgroup of $\mathrm{SU}(d)$ (checked against the Blichfeldt classification caps).
\end{enumerate}
The Sawicki--Karnas criterion is stated on $\mathcal{S}$, but the irreducibility of $\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{S}$ has a convenient sufficient check on $\mathcal{C}$ alone. Since $\mathcal{C}\subset\mathcal{S}$, every matrix commuting with $\mathrm{Ad}_s$ for all $s\in\mathcal{S}$ also commutes with $\mathrm{Ad}_c$ for all $c\in\mathcal{C}$, so $\mathrm{Comm}(\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{S})\subseteq\mathrm{Comm}(\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{C})$; if $\dim\mathrm{Comm}(\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{C})=1$ already, then $\dim\mathrm{Comm}(\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{S})=1$ for every $g$. Condition~(i) is therefore \emph{automatic} whenever $\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{C}$ is irreducible. Schur's orthogonality relation reduces the check on $\mathcal{C}$ to an $\mathcal{O}(d^2)$-memory character sum,
\begin{equation}
  \dim \mathrm{Comm}(\mathrm{Ad}_{\mathcal{C}}) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}|} \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} \bigl|\chi_\mathrm{Ad}(c)\bigr|^2\,,
  \label{eq:comm_dim}
\end{equation}
where $\chi_\mathrm{Ad}(c) = \mathrm{tr}\,\mathrm{Ad}_c$. When $\dim\mathrm{Comm}(\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{C})>1$, condition~(i) on $\mathcal{S}$ can still hold if $g$ mixes $\mathcal{C}$'s $\mathrm{Ad}$-invariant subspaces; in this case we re-check on the full generating set $\mathcal{C}\cup\{g, g^{-1}\}$ via the joint kernel of the $(\mathrm{Ad}_s\otimes I-I\otimes\mathrm{Ad}_s^\top)$ stack, valid on non-closed sets. Bases with reducible $\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{C}$ (e.g.\ $\Sigmathree{36}$, $\Sigma(60)\simeq A_5$, the $s_{60\times 4}$ family) are not automatically excluded; we defer them in this paper for compute reasons (\cref{sec:d3}) but flag them as natural targets for a follow-up. Condition~(ii) is enforced at finite cost by running a bounded closure $\langle \mathcal{S} \rangle \cap \mathrm{Cap}(n)$ against the classification of finite subgroups of $\mathrm{SU}(d)$ (orders $\leq 120$ at $d=2$, $\leq 1080$ at $d=3$, $\leq 5040$ at $d=4$)~\cite{Blichfeldt:1917,Fairbairn:1964,Flyvbjerg:1985,HananyHe:2001,GrimusLudl:2010}; we use cap sizes $n = 120, 1080, 5040$ respectively. An extension is flagged \texttt{finite} if closure terminates below the cap, \texttt{universal\_likely} if closure survives the cap and condition~(i) holds, and \texttt{infinite\_but\_not\_dense} if condition~(i) fails but closure is open.

\subsection{Candidate extensions}\label{sec:qco_candidates}

At $d=2$ the canonical extension of the Clifford group is $T = P(\pi/4) = \mathrm{diag}(1,\,e^{i\pi/4})$, giving the Clifford$+T$ set with certified synthesis via Ross--Selinger~\cite{Ross:2014kbr} and Kliuchnikov--Maslov--Mosca~\cite{Kliuchnikov:2012pla}; different base groups call for different extensions --- the Parzanchevski--Sarnak super-golden gates $T_{24}$ on Clifford and $T_{12}$ on the projective $A_4$ base~\cite{ParzanchevskiSarnak:2018} saturate $\Popt$ at $d=2$ on their respective bases.\footnote{Explicit matrix forms (\mbox{SU}(2)-normalized): $T_{24} \propto\left(\begin{smallmatrix}-1-\sqrt{2} & 2-\sqrt{2}+i \\ 2-\sqrt{2}-i & 1+\sqrt{2}\end{smallmatrix}\right)$, $T_{12} \propto\left(\begin{smallmatrix}3 & 1-i \\ 1+i & -3\end{smallmatrix}\right)$; both live in $\mathbb{Z}[i,\sqrt{2}]$.} We also consider diagonal $P(\theta)$ for $\theta \in \{\pi/8, 2\pi/9, \pi/9, \pi/18\}$ and \emph{arbitrary-SU($d$)} extensions --- unconstrained single-qudit unitaries sampled uniformly from the (bi-invariant) Haar measure on SU$(d)$, which we use as a generic-extension baseline; we label these rows \texttt{rnd} in the tables below.

At prime $d$ the Howard--Vala family~\cite{Howard:2012fva} generalizes $T$ via three integers $(z,\gamma,\varepsilon) \in \mathbb{Z}_d^{\,3}$. For example at $d=3$ the $\det=1$ condition requires a global $\omega^{1/3}$ twist, so the diagonal entries live on $9$th (not $3$rd) roots of unity~\cite[Eq.~27]{Howard:2012fva}:
\begin{equation}
  \HV{z}{\gamma}{\varepsilon} \;=\; \mathcal{N}\cdot \mathrm{diag}\!\left(1,\; \zeta_9^{v_1},\; \zeta_9^{v_2}\right),
  \label{eq:HV_def}
\end{equation}
with $\zeta_9 = e^{2\pi i/9}$, $v_1 = (6z + 2\gamma + 3\varepsilon)\bmod 9$, $v_2 = (6z + \gamma + 6\varepsilon)\bmod 9$, and $\mathcal{N}$ a phase fixing $\det\HV{z}{\gamma}{\varepsilon}=1$. (For primes $p>3$ the base root is $\zeta_p$ and $v_k$ is the cubic-in-$k$ recurrence solution $v_k = (k/12)\,[\,\gamma + k(6z + (2k{-}3)\gamma)\,] + k\varepsilon \pmod{p}$~\cite[Eq.~24]{Howard:2012fva}.) Our panel uses $(z,\gamma,\varepsilon) \in \{(1,1,0),\,(1,2,0),\,(2,1,0)\}$: the first is Campbell's magic gate~\cite{Campbell:2012jna}, the second is the Howard--Vala Eq.~(27) variant (Clifford-equivalent to the first at $d=3$~\cite{Howard:2012fva} --- hence identical $\delta$ in \cref{tab:d3_tier1}), and the third is a non-Clifford-equivalent orbit. The full group $\{U_\upsilon\}$ at $d=3$ factorizes as $\mathbb{Z}_9\times\mathbb{Z}_3$ modulo phases~\cite[Eq.~35]{Howard:2012fva}; these three samples cover two of the Clifford-equivalence orbits.

\section{SWIFTbot pipeline}\label{sec:methods}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\SWIFTbot is a five-stage agentic pipeline built around a heavily modified version of the Słowik--Dulián--Sawicki QCO kernel~\cite{Slowik:2025qco}: we retain the $t$-design $\delta$-evaluation core but add on-disk $\pi_\lambda(\mathcal{C})$ caching, a conjugation-based rewriting that replaces $|\mathcal{C}|$ exponentials with one, a Python subprocess driver with a persistent SQLite cache, and the Sawicki--Karnas pre-screen, QEC-code catalog, and distillation-protocol catalog that surround the numerics. Numerical stages are deterministic; an LLM supervisor intervenes only at stage boundaries to rank candidates, propose extensions, and decide when to stop. All headline numbers below come from deterministic panels that bypass the LLM; the agent is used for exploratory search not reported here. Full code and a command-line driver are released with the paper~\cite{swiftbot_github}. \cref{fig:pipeline} summarizes the stage-to-stage flow.

\emph{Stage~1 (group registration)} computes projective closures $\langle\mathcal{G}\rangle\subset\mathrm{SU}(d)/Z(\mathrm{SU}(d))$ from seed unitaries and stores each group under a content-addressable key. The registry covers the canonical $d\in\{2,3,4\}$ subgroups used here: Pauli--Clifford, the projective $A_4\subset\mathrm{PU}(2)$ (registered as \texttt{hurwitz}), and $2I$ (\texttt{BI}) at $d=2$; the $\Sigma(m\!\times\!3)$ series for $m\in\{36,72,216,360\}$ at $d=3$; and selected Fairbairn subgroups at $d=4$.

\emph{Stage~2 (universality screen)} evaluates $\dim\mathrm{Comm}(\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{C})$ via \cref{eq:comm_dim} and runs a bounded closure against the classification caps $\{120,1080,5040\}$~\cite{Blichfeldt:1917,Fairbairn:1964,Flyvbjerg:1985,HananyHe:2001,GrimusLudl:2010}. Pairs whose closure terminates below cap are tagged \texttt{finite} and skipped; reducible-adjoint pairs are tagged diagnostically.

\emph{Stage~3 (QCO numerics)} invokes the kernel of~\cite{Slowik:2025qco} as a subprocess. Two optimizations help: $(i)$ every gate has the form $g c g^\dagger$ with $c\in\mathcal{C}$, so $\pi_\lambda(g c g^\dagger) = \pi_\lambda(g)\pi_\lambda(c)\pi_\lambda(g)^\dagger$ replaces $|\mathcal{C}|$ exponentials with one; $(ii)$ the $\pi_\lambda(c)$ lists are persisted to disk keyed by a fingerprint of $\mathcal{C}$ and the highest weight $\lambda$. Together these give $3\text{--}6\times$ speedup at $d=3$. Results are stored as per-sample \texttt{QTRecord}s in a SQLite cache with full provenance.

\emph{Stages~4--5 (fault-tolerance)} match each surviving $(\mathcal{C},g)$ against a curated catalog of QEC codes (Steane, Reed--Muller, Bravyi--Haah, Kubischta 2I, qutrit triorthogonal, ternary Golay, Pollatsek--Ruskai PI, Kubischta--Teixeira PI, and more) and distillation protocols (Bravyi--Kitaev, Bravyi--Haah, Campbell--Anwar--Browne, programmable MSD, Ouyang--Jing--Brennen (OJB) code-switching). The catalog is curated for tractability, not exhaustive; Hastings--Haah sublogarithmic-overhead codes~\cite{HastingsHaah:2018} and the full Litinski resource landscape~\cite{Litinski:2019cheap} are notable out-of-scope omissions that would be natural additions. Unmatched pairs return a \emph{research-needed} marker with an EC Zoo~\cite{ecc_zoo} pointer rather than guessing.

\section{Validation at $d=2$}\label{sec:d2}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We validate the \SWIFTbot QCO kernel against the $d=2$ reference dataset of S{\l}owik, Duli{\'a}n, and Sawicki~\cite{Slowik:2025qco} on two qubit bases: the Clifford group ($|\mathcal{C}|=24$) and the $12$-element projective $A_4\subset\mathrm{PU}(2)$ (registered \texttt{hurwitz}), each paired with $P(\pi/4)$ and with arbitrary-SU(2) extensions. We also run a systematic $10^4$-sample search on the binary icosahedral group $2I$ (\texttt{BI}, $|\mathcal{C}|=120$), which carries the Kubischta nonadditive transversal code~\cite{Kubischta:2I}. All runs are at $t=50$, matching the reference. A zeroth-order check---replaying the $\delta$-parser on all $5050$ rows of the reference file and recomputing $\QT$---matches the reference to $\mathcal{O}(10^{-15})$, isolating any later disagreement to the spectral kernel. Headline results are summarized in \cref{tab:d2_validation}: Clifford$+P(\pi/4)$ yields $\QT=51.9$ (reference $\approx 52$); arbitrary-SU(2) extensions agree at the few-percent level.

\emph{Two remarks.} First, Clifford$+T$ has $\QT$ roughly $14\times$ \emph{larger} than the arbitrary-SU(2) baseline ($51.9$ vs $3.79$)---i.e.\ its approximation-exponent overhead is worse by an order of magnitude; commensurability between $P(\pi/4)$ and the Clifford orbit slows the random walk~\cite{Slowik:2025qco}. This is a $\QT$ claim, not a verdict on Clifford$+T$ itself: when the target is structured (\cref{sec:lamm}), the ranking inverts. Second, the \texttt{BI} ratio $\QT/\Popt \approx 1.27$ at $10^4$ samples is far closer to saturation than Clifford's and arrives with a cataloged fault-tolerant code~\cite{Kubischta:2I}; the empirical $\delta$ distribution on \texttt{BI} has median $0.38$ ($\QT\simeq 4.97$) and $5$th-percentile $0.31$, and a $2000$-resample bootstrap on the best-of-$10^4$ $\QT$ gives a tight $95\%$ CI of $[3.57, 3.69]$. The best-of-$10^4$ floor is the natural super-golden candidate predicted by~\cite{Sarnak:2015}.

\section{$d=3$ survey}\label{sec:d3}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

With the \SWIFTbot kernel validated at $d=2$ (\cref{sec:d2}), we turn to the first systematic $\QT$ ranking at $d=3$. The survey covers the three irreducible-adjoint $\Sigma$-series subgroups of $\mathrm{SU}(3)$---$\Sigmathree{72}$, $\Sigmathree{216}$, and $\Sigmathree{360}$---each extended by a common panel of eight candidate gates. (We use the $\Sigma(m\!\times\!3)$ notation of~\cite{Fairbairn:1964,GrimusLudl:2010}, where $|\Sigma(m\!\times\!3)| = 3m$; the three bases thus have orders $216$, $648$, $1080$.) The adjoints are irreducible --- the $8$-dimensional su$(3)$ representation does not split --- by the character tables of~\cite{GrimusLudl:2010}. The smaller groups $\Sigmathree{36}$ (the $108$-element crystallographic Valentiner analogue) and $\Sigma(60)\simeq A_5$ both have reducible adjoint at $d=3$: the $8$-dimensional su$(3)$ representation splits into smaller irreps of the subgroup, as can be read directly from the character tables~\cite{GrimusLudl:2010} or verified via \cref{eq:comm_dim} with $\dim\mathrm{Comm}(\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{C})>1$. These are not automatically non-universal: the $\Sigma$-invariant subspaces of su$(3)$ are not aligned with the Cartan--root decomposition (the Fourier element $V \in \Sigmathree{36}$ mixes them), so even a generic diagonal extension lifts $\dim\mathrm{Comm}(\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{S})$ to $1$ and the Sawicki--Karnas criterion is satisfied on $\mathcal{S}$. We defer $\Sigmathree{36}$ and $A_5$ from this paper purely for compute reasons --- the Tier-1/Tier-2 budget was set by the three irreducible-adjoint $\Sigma$-series bases --- and flag a $\Sigmathree{36}$/$A_5$ panel as natural follow-up work.\footnote{TODO: insert $\Sigmathree{36}+\{$panel$\}$ and $A_5+\{$panel$\}$ Q$_T$ data when compute is available; the SWIFTbot Stage-2 screen now classifies $\Sigmathree{36}$+rnd as \texttt{universal\_likely} via the corrected $\mathrm{Comm}(\mathrm{Ad}_\mathcal{S})$ check on generators.}

\subsection{Panel specification}\label{sec:d3_setup}

The panel fixes eight extensions per base group, with identical parameters across the three groups. Three are Howard--Vala variants $\HV{1}{1}{0}$, $\HV{1}{2}{0}$, $\HV{2}{1}{0}$ (see \cref{eq:HV_def}). Three are diagonal phase gates $P(\theta)$ at angles $\theta \in \{2\pi/9,\,\pi/9,\,2\pi/5\}$, chosen to span both the cyclotomic-$9$th-root sector relevant to \cref{sec:lamm} and the coarser $2\pi/5$ angle that generates no low-order closure against any of the three base groups. The remaining two slots per group are independent arbitrary-SU(3) batches of size $10$, drawn freshly per subprocess from the Haar measure on SU(3); these serve as the \emph{rnd} baseline of~\cite{Slowik:2025qco}. All eight extensions survive the Stage~2 universality screen of \cref{sec:qco_sawicki}: each $\Sigma$-series base has one-dimensional adjoint commutant by \cref{eq:comm_dim}, and the bounded closures stay open past the $|\mathcal{C}|\leq 1080$ cap of~\cite{Blichfeldt:1917,Fairbairn:1964,Flyvbjerg:1985,HananyHe:2001,GrimusLudl:2010}.

The panel is evaluated in two passes. An initial Tier-1 screen at $t=5$ --- the largest design order at which the full $3\,{\times}\,8$ panel completes in $\sim\!17$ min on a single $32$-core workstation --- fixes the ranking and feeds \cref{sec:d3_apples,sec:d3_ternary} below. For the headline values of \cref{tab:d3_tier1} and \cref{fig:qt_vs_groupsize} we re-run the panel at $t=10$ (Tier-2); all structured-extension $\delta$ agree bit-for-bit with $t=5$ except $\HV{2}{1}{0}$ on the two smaller groups, which lifts off its finite-$t$ floor at $t=10$ (see item (i) below). Fixed extensions use $\mathrm{sample\_size}=1$ (the gate is deterministic); rnd batches use $\mathrm{sample\_size}=10$. Each (group, extension) pair is dispatched as an independent \texttt{qco} subprocess with \texttt{OMP\_NUM\_THREADS=1} and \texttt{-{}-workers 8} to keep the per-irrep spectral evaluation of \cref{eq:delta_def} single-threaded while exploiting thread-level parallelism across the $|\mathcal{C}|$ matrix actions described in \cref{sec:methods}. All numerical values reported below are reproduced bit-identically from the SQLite caches released with the paper~\cite{swiftbot_github}.

\cref{fig:qt_vs_groupsize} elaborates three findings against \cref{tab:d3_tier1}.

\emph{Finding~1 (structured-extension degeneracy).} Five of six structured extensions give $\delta$ identical to four decimals across all three base groups, bit-for-bit the same as at $t=5$. $\HV{2}{1}{0}$ is the only exception: it lifts from the $t=5$ floor $\delta = 0.5000$ on the smaller groups to $\delta \approx 0.58$ at $t=10$, while $\Sigmathree{360}$ stays at $0.5698$. The degeneracy is structural, not a finite-$t$ artifact.

\emph{Finding~2 (linear $\QT$ growth with $\log|\mathcal{C}|$).} Because $\delta$ is essentially constant, $\QT$ grows linearly with $\log|\mathcal{C}|$ for the degenerate extensions: a larger base group needs proportionally more depth to densely cover $\mathrm{SU}(3)$, so the normalized $\QT/\log|\mathcal{C}|$ (rightmost column) collapses the three groups onto the same value.

\emph{Finding~3 (arbitrary-SU(3) gains with $|\mathcal{C}|$).} Best-sample rnd $\delta$ drops from $0.26$ to $0.18$ from $\Sigmathree{72}$ to $\Sigmathree{360}$; an arbitrary-SU(3) draw couples to every Lie-algebra direction including the subdominant channels that grow with $|\mathcal{C}|$. Statistically significant rnd distributions are deferred to \cref{sec:d3_apples,sec:d3_ternary}.

\subsection{Random-diagonal ternary scan}\label{sec:d3_ternary}

The Tier-1 and Tier-2 panels show a striking feature: five of six structured diagonal and Howard--Vala extensions give $\delta$ values identical to four decimals across $\Sigmathree{72}$ and $\Sigmathree{216}$. A natural question is whether this four-decimal degeneracy is specific to the cyclotomic content of the panel's three phases $P(2\pi/9), P(\pi/9), P(2\pi/5)$, or whether it applies to the full maximal torus of $\mathrm{SU}(3)$. We answer by scanning $N=50$ random $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ diagonal extensions $\mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta_1}, e^{i\theta_2}, e^{i\theta_3})$ (\texttt{seed}\,$=42$, $\theta_1, \theta_2$ uniform on $[0,2\pi)$, $\theta_3 = -\theta_1-\theta_2 \mod 2\pi$) on all three base groups at $t=5$, a $3 \times 50 = 150$-evaluation panel completed in $\sim\!14$ minutes of wall time on a 32-core node.

The result is stronger than the structured-panel observation: for every one of the $50$ random diagonals, the $\delta$ value on $\Sigmathree{72}$ and $\Sigmathree{216}$ agrees \emph{bit-for-bit}, while $\Sigmathree{360}$ is generically distinct. Per-group summary statistics (best / mean / max) across the $50$ samples are
\begin{IEEEeqnarray}{rCl}
\Sigmathree{72},\ \Sigmathree{216} \ &:& \ 0.4516 \ / \ 0.6769 \ / \ 0.9921, \\
\Sigmathree{360}\ &:& \ 0.2539 \ / \ 0.5106 \ / \ 0.9662.
\end{IEEEeqnarray}
\cref{fig:d3_rnd_diag_ternary} plots the full distribution on the SU(3) maximal-torus simplex; the $\Sigma$-series base groups have a permutation subgroup (the qutrit Clifford contains SU(3) Weyl elements), so each random-diagonal point is $S_3$-symmetrized to six positions with the same $\delta$, exposing the full torus symmetry.

The Finding-1 degeneracy is therefore \emph{not} a property of the cyclotomic angles we chose to probe but a structural property of the nested-subgroup relation $\Sigmathree{72} \subset \Sigmathree{216}$~\cite{GrimusLudl:2010}: at $t=5$, the dominant $t$-design irreps of $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ restrict to representations that both groups act on identically, so any diagonal extension couples through the same spectral channel on both. $\Sigmathree{360}$ is not a supergroup of either (it contains $A_5$ rather than the $\Sigmathree{72}$ center-extended chain~\cite{GrimusLudl:2010}), hence its breakout. A $t=10$ rerun would test whether a subdominant irrep of $\Sigmathree{216}$ that is absent in $\Sigmathree{72}$ becomes spectrally visible and lifts the degeneracy.

\subsection{Best achievable $\QT$}\label{sec:d3_best}

Combining the two panels, the best (group, extension) pair is \texttt{apple04} on $\Sigmathree{360}$ at $t=5$ with $\delta = 0.1197$ and $\QT = 3.291$---within $32\%$ of $\Popt = 2.495$. Note that this rnd winner, like every arbitrary-SU(d) extension, is not exactly synthesized and therefore inherits the distillation caveat of \cref{sec:discussion_ft}: the $\QT$ figure ranks reachability, not fault-tolerant cost. At $t=10$ the best \emph{fixed} extension is $\HV{2}{1}{0}$ on $\Sigmathree{72}$, with $\QT = 10.28$, a factor $3.8$ above $\Popt = 2.692$. The shape of the ranking is the same at both $t$: arbitrary-SU(3) extensions dominate every structured extension, $\HV{2}{1}{0}$ leads the structured field, and the three diagonal-phase angles $P(2\pi/9), P(\pi/9), P(2\pi/5)$ all give worse $\QT$ than even the worst Howard--Vala variant. The narrow-angle $P(\pi/9)$ in particular produces $\delta$ within $3\%$ of unity, inflating $\QT$ by two orders of magnitude relative to the arbitrary-SU(3) baseline---a direct analogue of the $14\times$ Clifford$+T$ penalty at $d=2$ (\cref{sec:d2}).

The Tier-1 ($t=5$) and Tier-2 ($t=10$) panels agree to four decimals for all structured extensions except the $\HV{2}{1}{0}$ floor lift noted above; the $d=2$ Clifford$+T$ $\QT(t)$ curve of~\cite{Slowik:2025qco} reaches the same stabilization pattern by $t\gtrsim 30$, so our $t=5$/$t=10$ cross-check is the $d=3$ analogue. The four-decimal cross-group $\delta$-degeneracy is therefore not a finite-$t$ artifact; it is a structural feature of rational-angle diagonal and Howard--Vala extensions on the $\Sigma$-series. Rnd batches at $t=10$ are single-sample draws (the qco optimized kernel aggregates to one row per subprocess regardless of the \texttt{sample\_size} argument); statistically significant Haar statistics come from the apples-100 panel in \cref{sec:d3_apples} and from the $10^4$-sample \texttt{BI} pilot at $d=2$ (\cref{sec:d2}).

\section{The DSA primitive-gate target family}\label{sec:lamm}

The $d=3$ survey of \cref{sec:d3} ranks extensions by the Haar-uniform figure of merit $\QT$. Many applications do not need a uniform net. Discrete-subgroup approximations of lattice gauge simulations~\cite{Lamm:2019bik,Alam:2021uuq,Gustafson:2022xdt} require instead a small structured family of unitaries: a few continuous-angle $R_Z$ rotations with group-theoretic coefficients, plus a finite set of cyclotomic phases from the discrete Fourier transform $U_F$. On such a workload the relevant figure of merit is the mean synthesis cost over that finite family, not a $t$-design spectral gap. The two figures of merit disagree in general. We note that $\Sigmathree{36}$ itself appears here as a \emph{target family} of SU(2) rotations derived from its trace spectrum under a qubit encoding~\cite{GustafsonJi:2024sig}; it is not used as a base group, its reducible adjoint at $d=3$ having excluded it from the QCO survey (\cref{sec:d3}).

\paragraph*{A concrete target family.}
We work out the case of the $\Sigmathree{36}$ encoding as a representative example; different subgroups in the series produce different coefficient sets. The trace gate $U_\mathrm{Tr}(\theta) = \mathrm{diag}_{g \in \mathcal{C}}\bigl(e^{i \theta\,\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{Tr}(g)}\bigr)$ appears in the magnetic plaquette of any DSA lattice gauge simulation~\cite{Lamm:2019bik}. For $\Sigmathree{36}$, Gustafson~\emph{et~al.}~\cite{GustafsonJi:2024sig} reduce it to a three-qubit trace register: the $14$ conjugacy classes collapse to $7$ distinct $\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{Tr}(g)\in\{3,\pm 1,\pm\tfrac{1}{2},-\tfrac{3}{2},0\}$, and a Boolean map $(p,q,r,s,t)\mapsto(v_0,v_1,v_2)$ separates them. On the $(v_0,v_1,v_2)$ register the trace Hamiltonian factorizes as
\begin{equation}
  H_\mathrm{Tr} = \tfrac{1}{16}\bigl[3\,(III{+}IIZ{+}IZI) + 5\,IZZ + 1\,ZII + 9\,(ZIZ{+}ZZI) + 15\,ZZZ\bigr],
\end{equation}
so $U_\mathrm{Tr}(\theta)$ decomposes (up to Clifford conjugation) into $R_Z$ rotations at angles proportional to $\alpha_c\theta$ for $\alpha_c \in \{1,3,5,9,15\}$ --- the five distinct non-zero coefficients in the decomposition. These coefficients are specific to $\Sigmathree{36}$; other subgroups in the series produce different trace spectra and different $\alpha_c$. The discrete half comes from $U_F$: up to global phase, the Howard--Vala qutrit gate $\HV{1}{2}{0}$~\cite{Howard:2012fva} injects $9$th-root phases, requiring $R_Z(2\pi k/9)$ for $k=1,\ldots,8$. The synthesis target for the $\Sigmathree{36}$ qubit pipeline is therefore
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{T}_{36} = \bigl\{R_Z(2\pi k/9)\bigr\}_{k=1}^{8} \cup \bigl\{R_Z(\alpha_c\,\theta)\bigr\}_{\alpha_c \in \{1,3,5,9,15\},\, \theta \in (0,\pi)}.
  \label{eq:T36}
\end{equation}

\paragraph*{Mean-depth figure of merit.}
Given a qubit base group $\mathcal{C}$ and single-gate extension $g$, \SWIFTbot enumerates the word tree of $\mathcal{C}\cup\{g,g^{-1}\}$ by breadth-first search with projective-phase deduplication, capped at $2\times 10^5$ unique words. For each $U\in\mathcal{T}_{36}$ we record the shortest BFS word whose projective distance $d(U,W) = \sqrt{2 - |\mathrm{Tr}(U^\dagger W)|}$ falls below $\varepsilon = 10^{-2}$, or the depth of closest approach if no such word exists. The $\varepsilon = 10^{-2}$ floor matches the per-link accuracy typical of near-term Trotter budgets~\cite{Lamm:2019bik}; the BFS-exhaust cap prevents runaway search at this tolerance, and tightening $\varepsilon$ can only increase the $6.8\times$ advantage (see \S Caveats below). Discrete targets may be hit \emph{exactly} at finite depth, in which case the corresponding distance is zero and the $T$-count is read directly from the word. Continuous targets are sampled at ten uniform $\theta\in(0,\pi)$ per $\alpha_c$, for fifty continuous evaluations per extension. We report mean distance across \cref{eq:T36} as the single headline number; ties are broken on the discrete-exact count.

\paragraph*{Result.}
\cref{tab:lamm_sigma36} ranks the candidates at BFS depth $15$, $\varepsilon = 10^{-2}$, $N_\theta = 10$ per continuous coefficient. The Clifford$+P(2\pi/9)$ extension attains mean projective distance $0.0122$ on $\mathcal{T}_{36}$ with $27$ of $58$ targets hit below $\varepsilon$, while the canonical Clifford$+T$ extension attains mean distance $0.0833$ with only $5$ hits---a factor $6.8\times$ separation in mean distance. The equivalence class of $P(2\pi k/18)$ with $\gcd(k,18)=1$ (which includes $P(\pi/9)$ and $P(\pi/18)$) all saturate the discrete $9$th-root targets, a direct consequence of $\langle \mathrm{Clifford}, P(2\pi/9)\rangle$ closing at a finite group of $\sim\! 3\times 10^4$ elements; the algebraic ring $\mathbb{Z}[\omega_{18}, \tfrac{1}{2}]$ supplies every $9$th-root phase via a short word. Within this equivalence class, Clifford$+P(\pi/18)$ marginally edges Clifford$+P(2\pi/9)$ at depth $15$ (mean $0.0104$ vs.\ $0.0122$; $34$ vs.\ $27$ hits), reflecting the strictly larger $18$th-root coverage: $P(\pi/18)$ generates $\omega_{36}$, which contains both the $9$th- and $18$th-root phases of $U_F$. Crucially, the super-golden extensions $T_{24}$ and $T_{12}$---which optimize $\QT$ for Clifford and for the $12$-element projective $A_4$ base \texttt{hurwitz} respectively~\cite{Sarnak:2015}---perform \emph{worse} on $\mathcal{T}_{36}$ than the rational-angle rows: their algebra $\mathbb{Z}[i,\sqrt{2}]$ does not contain $\omega_9$, so they cannot reach the $9$th-root targets exactly at any depth; on the $|\mathcal{C}|=12$ \texttt{hurwitz} base the $T_{12}$ extension hits zero targets at depth $15$. The \texttt{hurwitz}$+P(\pi/18)$ row is competitive ($35$ hits, mean $0.0112$) and does so with a strictly \emph{smaller} base group: in the target-family regime the overhead advantage tracks the cyclotomic content of the extension, not the size of the base.

\paragraph*{Interpretation.}
Clifford$+P(2\pi/9)$ is worse on $\QT$ than Clifford$+T$ yet $6.8\times$ better on mean depth over $\mathcal{T}_{36}$. The reason is structural: $\QT$ is the coefficient of a Haar-uniform covering rate, while $\mathcal{T}_{36}$ is a measure-zero algebraic slice of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ whose $\omega_9$ cyclotomic content matches $\langle\mathrm{Clifford}, P(2\pi/9)\rangle$. The super-golden extensions saturate $\Popt$ but equidistribute in the wrong direction for this target. A lattice-gauge simulator compiling \cref{eq:T36} should therefore rank extensions by target-family mean depth, not by $\QT$, and the two rankings can disagree by nearly an order of magnitude.

\paragraph*{Caveats.}
The Clifford$+T$ row's $T$-count is certified by Ross--Selinger exact synthesis~\cite{Ross:2014kbr,Kliuchnikov:2012pla}; the companion Clifford-cyclotomic framework of Forest~\emph{et~al.}~\cite{Forest:2015cc} extends this to $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_{2^n}]$ but not to the rational-prime-denominator rings relevant here. No analogous normal form exists for $\mathbb{Z}[\omega_{18},\tfrac{1}{2}]$, so the rational-angle rows carry depth-$15$ BFS upper bounds. The $6.8\times$ advantage is therefore a \emph{lower bound}: a certified synthesis theorem over $\mathbb{Z}[\omega_{18},\tfrac{1}{2}]$ would only shorten the BFS-side words, while the canonical Ross--Selinger side is already tight. Anderson--Jochym-O'Connor~\cite{Anderson:2014} excludes any qubit \emph{stabilizer} code with transversal $|k\pi/9\rangle$. Two routes sidestep this. Programmable MSD~\cite{DuclosCianciPoulin:2015,CampbellOGorman:2016,CampbellHoward:2017} is indirect synthesis: it prepares $|\psi(\theta)\rangle$ from already-distilled $|T\rangle$ on a Clifford$+T$ stabilizer substrate, so the AJOC no-go never applied to it. Non-stabilizer code-switching to a Kubischta--Teixeira PI code~\cite{KubischtaTeixeira:2024,OuyangJingBrennen:2024} is a genuine bypass but requires a PI code of sufficient distance hosting $|\omega_9\rangle$, which has not been constructed. The $6.8\times$ advantage is cleanest when $P(2\pi/9)$ is a native primitive---e.g., in a qutrit register or on a hypothetical nonadditive code that distills $|\omega_9\rangle$ directly.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Two findings drive the discussion below. First, at $d=3$ and $t=5$, arbitrary-SU(3) extensions on the largest irreducible-adjoint $\Sigma$-series base reach $\QT/\Popt \approx 1.32$---essentially the same ``golden-gate direction'' observed at $d=2$, where the arbitrary-SU(2) baseline sits $4\%$ above $\Popt$ on the $12$-element \texttt{hurwitz} base (\cref{sec:d2}). No structured extension in our panel (Howard--Vala or rational diagonal) gets closer than a factor $\sim\!2.9$ of $\Popt$. Second, and in apparent tension with the first, the non-canonical Clifford$+P(2\pi/9)$ extension beats the canonical Clifford$+T$ by $6.8\times$ on the DSA primitive-gate target family (\cref{sec:lamm}) despite being $\sim\!8\times$ worse on $\QT$. The two findings together are the paper's central design lesson: the ``best general-purpose extension'' and the ``best target-specific extension'' optimize different objectives, and systematic qudit fault-tolerance design must report both.

\subsection{Fault-tolerance status of the winning extensions}\label{sec:discussion_ft}

A $\QT$ ranking is necessary but not sufficient for fault-tolerant implementation. \cref{tab:distillation} audits every extension evaluated against the curated \SWIFTbot catalogs of transversal codes, distillation protocols, and certified synthesis rings. Only canonical Clifford$+T$ at $d=2$ admits an end-to-end certified stack and serves as our validation anchor. Three rows inherit a transversal-code half only: \texttt{hurwitz}-based super-golden extensions via Parzanchevski--Sarnak~\cite{ParzanchevskiSarnak:2018}, \texttt{BI}$+\mathrm{rnd}$ via the Kubischta 2I code~\cite{Kubischta:2I}, and the $d=3$ $\Sigma+\HV{z}{\gamma}{\varepsilon}$ rows via the qutrit triorthogonal $[[9m-k,k,2]]_3$ family~\cite{Campbell:2012jna}. All other rows lack either a distillation circuit or a certified synthesis ring.

Three regimes deserve comment. First, the cyclotomic ring $\mathbb{Z}[\omega_{18},\tfrac{1}{2}]$---hosting the $d=2$ target-family winners $P(2\pi/9)$, $P(\pi/9)$, $P(\pi/18)$---has no Ross--Selinger-style normal form, and the two non-stabilizer handling routes discussed in \cref{sec:lamm} Caveats (indirect programmable MSD on a Clifford$+T$ substrate; PI code-switching pending a suitable distance-preserving PI code) remain the best-known options at $d=2$. At $d=3$ the same rotations are qutrit-native and Campbell--Anwar--Browne~\cite{Campbell:2012jna} distills $|\omega_9\rangle$ directly.

Second, the super-golden gates of Parzanchevski--Sarnak~\cite{ParzanchevskiSarnak:2018} live in $\mathbb{Z}[i,\sqrt{2}]$ and are not Clifford-orbit rotations of stabilizer states, so triorthogonal distillation does not apply. Whether a golden-gate-compatible distillation circuit can be built---possibly on the Kubischta 2I code, whose transversal group is precisely the super-golden base---is open.

Third, arbitrary-SU(d) extensions are by definition not exactly synthesized, and so cannot be distilled in the usual sense~\cite{Bravyi:2004isx,Bravyi:2012czv}. This applies uniformly to every rnd row; the best current claim is transversal-group support at the base-code level.

For the $\Sigma$-series at $d=3$, data and distillation codes are structurally separable. The Howard--Vala magic state is distilled by the qutrit triorthogonal family~\cite{Campbell:2012jna,PrakashSaha:2025}, but no explicit data code hosts a $\Sigma$-series group transversally. Denys--Leverrier covariant codes at $d=2$~\cite{DenysLeverrier:2024,DenysLeverrier:2023} support $2T$ and $2O$ group actions natively through continuous-group covariance, distinct from Eastin--Knill transversality on a stabilizer code; extending either to a qutrit $\Sigma(m\!\times\!3)$ base---an open case inside the twisted unitary $t$-group program~\cite{KubischtaTeixeira:2024}---would complete the stack. (Clifford$+R$ synthesis~\cite{Gustafson:2025qutrit} separately closes the compilation-certification gap for these $d=3$ gate sets; we return to this below.)

Higher-dimensional qudit MSD is advancing rapidly. Krishna--Tillich~\cite{KrishnaTillich:2019} opened the low-overhead program; Wills, Hsieh, and Yamasaki~\cite{WillsHsiehYamasaki:2025} achieved the first constant-overhead MSD construction via asymptotically good quantum codes; Golowich--Guruswami~\cite{GolowichGuruswami:2024} and Nguyen~\cite{Nguyen:2025ccz} gave asymptotically good qubit codes with transversal non-Clifford and CCZ gates. Most directly applicable to our framework, Cervia~\emph{et~al.}~\cite{Cervia:2025agc} construct triorthogonal codes on $\mathbb{F}_{2^{2m}}$ at qudit dimensions as small as $q=64$ that beat the Tsfasman--Vlădu\c{t}--Zink bound and give constant-space-overhead $|CCZ\rangle$ distillation, together with an explicit qudit-to-qubit conversion circuit that makes the construction a drop-in upgrade for any Clifford+CCZ evaluation. None of these high-$d$ codes have cataloged transversal pairings with our base-group registry, but the infrastructure now exists to pair them.

A complementary recent development is \emph{magic-state cultivation}~\cite{Gidney:2024cultivation}: rather than distilling high-fidelity $|T\rangle$ states from many noisy copies, one grows a protected $|T\rangle$ inside a surface-code patch using roughly the same gate budget as a logical CNOT, reducing qubit-round cost by an order of magnitude at realistic noise~\cite{Vaknin:2025cultivation}, with an experimental demonstration on a superconducting processor~\cite{Rosenfeld:2025cultivation}. Cultivation is currently qubit-native and surface-code-specific; the open question for the qudit program is whether a native-$d$ analogue exists on codes with transversal non-Clifford structure (Kubischta 2I, Denys--Leverrier 2T/2O, or the twisted $t$-group codes of \cite{KubischtaTeixeira:2024}). Two arguments have historically favored $d=2$: the maturity of Ross--Selinger Clifford$+T$ synthesis and the qubit-specificity of magic-state cultivation. The first is closing at $d=3$ with Clifford$+R$ synthesis~\cite{Gustafson:2025qutrit}; a qudit cultivation analogue would close the second.

On the synthesis side of the qudit story, single-qutrit exact-synthesis methods over Clifford$+T$ and related Clifford+cyclotomic rings are by now well developed~\cite{Prakash:2018nqcl,Glaudell:2019qcanon,Kalra:2025qutrit,Gustafson:2025qutrit}, so the qutrit compilation story is closing to parity with the qubit one.

Our Tier-2 result that $\Sigmathree{216}$+$\HV{1}{1}{0}$ gives $\QT = 16.56$ on the qutrit Clifford base --- a factor $\sim\!6.5$ above the Kesten--McKay floor $\Popt = 2.54$ --- is a numerical shadow of a sharper number-theoretic result: Evra and Parzanchevski~\cite{EvraParzanchevski:2024cliffordT} prove that the qutrit Clifford$+T$ gate set generates a \emph{thin} matrix group in $\mathrm{PU}(3)$ (Zariski-dense but of infinite index in its ambient $S$-arithmetic group), so no near-optimal covering theorem applies and the covering rate is bounded away from the golden-gate exponent. Their Clifford$+D$ extension --- replacing the single $T$-gate with the diagonal subgroup $\mathcal{D} \cong (\mathbb{Z}/18)^3 \subset \mathrm{U}(3)$ of signed $9$th-root diagonals ($|\mathcal{D}| = 5832$ in $\mathrm{U}(3)$, or $324$ distinct elements in $\mathrm{PU}(3)$ after quotienting by the common phase) --- recovers arithmeticity and covers $\mathrm{PU}(3)$ within a factor $\log_3 105 \approx 4.24$ of optimal; and arithmetic-lattice constructions on Bruhat--Tits buildings~\cite{EvraParzanchevski:2022pu3} saturate the bound but live in infinite groups. Both are outside the single-gate $(\mathcal{C},g)$ framework of this paper: Clifford$+\mathcal{D}$ requires a set-valued generalization $(\mathcal{C},\mathcal{G})$, and the arithmetic-lattice golden gates are not finite subgroups at all.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:discussion_limits}

Three limitations frame the numerical claims. First, the $d=3$ Haar statistics come from either single-sample $t=10$ draws (Tier-2 panel) or the $100$-matrix apples-to-apples panel at $t=5$ (\cref{sec:d3_apples}); a joint $t=10$, $\sim\!100$-sample Haar run is held up by a qco-kernel limitation that aggregates each subprocess to a single output row regardless of sample size. Second, no $d=4$ survey is attempted: largest-irrep dimensions scale as $t^6$ at $d=4$ and squared matrix-storage as $t^{12}$, so only the smallest SU(4) subgroups ($|\mathcal{C}|\lesssim 300$) fit in a single-node 125\,GB budget at $t=10$. A pilot on those is tractable overnight; the large-$|\mathcal{C}|$ SU(4) subgroups (e.g.\ $\mathrm{S7F}$) would need a cluster slot. Third, the target-family $T$-counts in \cref{tab:lamm_sigma36} are depth-$15$ BFS upper bounds except for the Ross--Selinger-certified Clifford$+P(\pi/4)$ row. The $6.8\times$ advantage is a \emph{lower bound}: a certified normal form over $\mathbb{Z}[\omega_{18},\tfrac{1}{2}]$~\cite{Forest:2015cc} would only tighten the BFS side, not the canonical side, which is already exact.

\subsection{Open questions}\label{sec:discussion_open}

Two quantitative questions are directly posed by our $t=5$ data and should be sharp enough to settle at $t \geq 10$: (a) does the four-decimal $\delta$-degeneracy across $\Sigmathree{216,648,1080}$ observed for structured diagonal and Howard--Vala extensions in \cref{tab:d3_tier1} persist, break, or re-rank at larger $t$? and (b) does the $\Sigmathree{360}$ apples-panel mean cross strictly below the $\Sigmathree{216}$ mean once the $\sim\!100$-matrix apples panel removes the $10$-matrix outlier sensitivity? Question~(b) has been \emph{settled in the affirmative at $t=5$} in \cref{sec:d3_apples} ($5.7\sigma/\sqrt{N}$ separation at $N=100$); the $t\geq 10$ rerun will establish whether the same ordering holds at converged design order. Three qualitative questions are left open: (c) a super-golden construction at $d=3$ saturating $\Popt \approx 2.69$ on $\Sigmathree{72}$, $2.54$ on $\Sigmathree{216}$, or $2.50$ on $\Sigmathree{360}$; (d) an explicit data code $C_\mathrm{data}$ that hosts one of our $\Sigma$-series base groups transversally, analogous to the Denys--Leverrier constructions for $2T$ and $2O$ at $d=2$~\cite{DenysLeverrier:2024,DenysLeverrier:2023} or to the Kubischta--Teixeira twisted unitary $t$-group framework~\cite{KubischtaTeixeira:2024}; and (e) a Ross--Selinger-style normal form for the cyclotomic ring $\mathbb{Z}[\omega_{18},\tfrac{1}{2}]$ that would certify the \cref{tab:lamm_sigma36} $T$-counts.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Conclusions}\label{sec:conclusions}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\SWIFTbot---an agentic workflow that ranks $(\mathcal{C},g)$ pairs by $\QT$ and cross-references each against a curated fault-tolerance catalog---produces the first systematic $d=3$ $\QT$ ranking in our registry and identifies $\Sigmathree{360}$ with an arbitrary-SU(3) extension as the best general-purpose qudit base ($\QT/\Popt\approx 1.32$ in the apples-100 panel at $t=5$, and consistent single-sample draws at $t=10$). A systematic $10^4$-sample arbitrary-SU(2) pilot on the binary icosahedral base at $d=2$ gives $\QT/\Popt \approx 1.27$, the natural super-golden candidate. Applied to a lattice-gauge simulation target family, the non-canonical Clifford$+P(2\pi/9)$ beats canonical Clifford$+T$ by $6.8\times$ on mean depth despite being worse on $\QT$, establishing that the two rankings locate different gates. Only canonical Clifford$+T$ admits an end-to-end certified fault-tolerant stack; every other winner is covered partially, with sharp open algebraic-compilation or code-pairing problems standing between it and full fault-tolerance. Four concrete next steps carry most of the remaining design risk. (i) A $d=4$ pilot on the Fairbairn subgroups~\cite{Fairbairn:1964} would test whether the $\QT/\Popt \to 1$ trend continues past $d=3$ or the $d=3$ ``golden direction'' is an accident. (ii) An explicit $\Sigma$-series $\leftrightarrow$ data-code transversal pairing~\cite{DenysLeverrier:2024,KubischtaTeixeira:2024,OuyangJingBrennen:2024} would upgrade the $d=3$ winners from \textsc{partial} to \textsc{complete} in \cref{tab:distillation}. (iii) A Ross--Selinger normal form for $\mathbb{Z}[\omega_{18},\tfrac{1}{2}]$ would replace the BFS upper bound in \cref{tab:lamm_sigma36} and likely sharpen the $6.8\times$ headline. (iv) A \emph{set-valued} generalization of the framework from single-gate extensions $(\mathcal{C}, g)$ to gate-set extensions $(\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{G})$ would bring constructions like the Evra--Parzanchevski Clifford$+\mathcal{D}$ PU(3) golden gates~\cite{EvraParzanchevski:2024cliffordT} into the $\QT$ ranking --- empirically testing whether the $\log_3 105$ covering-rate factor is recovered on $\Sigmathree{216}+\mathcal{D}$, and plausibly exposing other set-valued near-optima that single-extension $\QT$ cannot see. For a hardware vendor choosing between qubit and qudit architectures \emph{today}, the practical takeaway is narrower: if the workload is lattice-gauge-simulation-shaped, a rational-phase qubit Clifford extension already buys nearly an order of magnitude over the canonical Clifford$+T$ stack with no new hardware.
